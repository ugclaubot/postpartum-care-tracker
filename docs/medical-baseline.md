# Medical Baseline

This document keeps the dashboard's recommendations conservative and source-traceable.

## Source Hierarchy

Use clinician-facing guidelines, consensus statements, and peer-reviewed literature before patient blogs. Patient/public-health pages can still be used for plain-language warning signs, but the dashboard's rules should be anchored to ACOG, NICE, WHO, ADA, FOGSI, ATA, NATA, PubMed-indexed reviews, or equivalent professional sources.

## Core Follow-up Windows

- Early pregnancy booking visit: baseline physical review plus CBC/platelets, blood group/Rh, antibody screen, urine routine/culture, infection screening, glucose risk, thyroid screening, and diet/risk-specific labs should be discussed promptly once pregnancy is suspected or confirmed.
- Early dating/viability ultrasound: first-trimester ultrasound is the key tool for confirming/revising pregnancy dating, location, viability, and multiples. WHO supports at least one ultrasound before 24 weeks; early ultrasound improves dating and anomaly/multiple-pregnancy detection.
- First-trimester fetal screening: discuss NIPT from 10 weeks and NT/dual-marker style screening around 11-13+6 weeks, with diagnostic CVS/amnio only when screening, ultrasound, or family history indicates it.
- Mid-pregnancy screening: detailed anomaly scan around 18-20+6 weeks; AFP/quad only when earlier screening was missed or clinician wants added neural-tube-risk screening.
- Diabetes screening: if early glucose testing is normal, routine gestational diabetes screening usually sits around 24-28 weeks.
- Third-trimester planning: repeat anemia/nutrient and Rh/antibody checks as indicated, growth scans only when risk-based, and birth-readiness checks around 36-37 weeks.
- First postpartum contact: within 3 weeks after birth, with ongoing care as needed and a comprehensive visit no later than 12 weeks.
- WHO-style postnatal contacts: first day, day 3, days 7-14, and around 6 weeks.
- Hypertension risk: blood pressure should be checked early, commonly within 3-10 days for those with hypertensive disorders or concerning symptoms.
- Gestational diabetes: glucose screening is recommended postpartum. ACOG mentions fasting plasma glucose or 75 g 2-hour OGTT at 4-12 weeks. FOGSI guidance for India recommends postpartum screening at 6-12 weeks for women who had GDM.
- Mental health: screen for perinatal depression and anxiety at postpartum visits.
- Positive or faint urine pregnancy test after delivery/pregnancy event: quantitative serum beta-hCG is the useful first blood test, repeated around 48 hours when pregnancy location/viability is not clear.

## Dashboard Interpretation Rules

Rules are prompts for follow-up, not diagnosis.

- No dashboard test plan can detect every possible pregnancy issue. The dashboard should separate universal screening, risk-based screening, symptom-triggered urgent evaluation, and tests that are not useful routinely.
- Early pregnancy hormone rules: TSH is a first-line early pregnancy test; Free T4 follows abnormal TSH or thyroid suspicion. Quantitative beta-hCG is useful for confirmation/trending when dating, ectopic risk, viability, or pregnancy location is unclear. Progesterone is conditional, not universal.
- AMH, FSH, LH, estradiol, prolactin, testosterone, DHEAS, and cortisol are not routine pregnancy-monitoring tests unless a specific endocrine disorder or symptom points to them.
- hCG alone cannot confirm pregnancy location. NICE pregnancy-of-unknown-location guidance uses two serum beta-hCG values about 48 hours apart: a rise greater than 63% suggests likely developing intrauterine pregnancy but ectopic cannot be excluded; a fall greater than 50% suggests the pregnancy is unlikely to continue; values outside those patterns need clinical review.
- Consider earlier transvaginal ultrasound discussion when serum beta-hCG is at or above 1,500 IU/L, or immediately with pain, heavy bleeding, fainting, shoulder-tip pain, or worsening symptoms.
- Published postpartum data report hCG usually disappearing during the third week after delivery; persistent positive hCG beyond that window needs clinician review for context such as new pregnancy, retained products, or other causes.
- Blood pressure at or above 160 systolic or 110 diastolic is treated as urgent.
- Blood pressure at or above 140 systolic or 90 diastolic prompts clinician contact, especially with headache, vision change, swelling, chest pain, or shortness of breath.
- Fever at or above 38 C is urgent in postpartum context.
- Heavy bleeding, chest pain, trouble breathing, seizure, fainting, severe headache, vision changes, one-sided leg swelling, or self-harm thoughts are urgent.
- EPDS-like depression score at 13 or above prompts clinician review. Any self-harm thought is urgent regardless of total score.
- Glucose values use common nonpregnant criteria for postpartum GDM follow-up discussion: fasting 100-125 mg/dL suggests impaired fasting glucose, 126 mg/dL or higher suggests diabetes-range; 2-hour OGTT 140-199 mg/dL suggests impaired glucose tolerance, 200 mg/dL or higher suggests diabetes-range. Confirm with clinician and lab.
- Ferritin can be falsely elevated during the first 6 weeks postpartum because it is an acute-phase reactant; interpret with CBC, symptoms, and timing.
- Hemoglobin, WBC, ferritin, serum iron, transferrin saturation, TSH, Free T4, vitamin D, B12, liver enzymes, platelets, urine protein/creatinine ratio, and creatinine should use the lab's postpartum/contextual reference ranges.

## Food and Supplement Guidance

The dashboard should suggest food patterns and clinician discussion, not dosing:

- Possible iron deficiency: discuss iron treatment; food support may include lentils, beans, greens, jaggery in moderation, meat or eggs if used, plus vitamin C with meals.
- Glucose risk: discuss a structured nutrition plan; food support may include protein and fiber at each meal, less sugary drinks, and post-meal walking when cleared.
- Breastfeeding or low vitamin D risk: discuss vitamin D, calcium, iodine, and B12 needs with clinician, especially for vegetarian or vegan diets.
- Thyroid risk: do not self-adjust thyroid medicine; ask about TSH and free T4 timing.
